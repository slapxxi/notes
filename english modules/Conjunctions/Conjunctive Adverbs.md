### **Comprehensive List of Conjunctive Adverbs Sorted by Popularity with Examples**

Conjunctive adverbs are words that connect independent clauses and show the relationship between ideas. They function as both adverbs and conjunctions, linking two clauses while also providing additional meaning, such as cause, effect, contrast, or sequence.

Here’s a comprehensive list of common conjunctive adverbs sorted by popularity, along with examples of their usage:

---

## **1. However**

- **Usage**: Indicates contrast or contradiction.
    
- **Example**: _The weather was cold; **however**, we still went for a walk._
    
- **Example**: _I studied hard; **however**, I didn't pass the exam._
    

---

## **2. Therefore**

- **Usage**: Indicates cause and effect, meaning "for that reason."
    
- **Example**: _She didn’t finish her homework; **therefore**, she couldn’t go out._
    
- **Example**: _I forgot my umbrella; **therefore**, I got wet._
    

---

## **3. Moreover**

- **Usage**: Adds additional information or emphasizes something.
    
- **Example**: _He is an excellent teacher; **moreover**, he is very kind._
    
- **Example**: _The meeting was productive; **moreover**, it was enjoyable._
    

---

## **4. Consequently**

- **Usage**: Indicates result or consequence.
    
- **Example**: _She didn’t study for the test; **consequently**, she failed._
    
- **Example**: _The roads were icy; **consequently**, the bus was delayed._
    

---

## **5. Thus**

- **Usage**: Indicates conclusion or result, similar to "therefore."
    
- **Example**: _The project was delayed; **thus**, the deadline was extended._
    
- **Example**: _He forgot to set his alarm; **thus**, he was late for work._
    

---

## **6. Meanwhile**

- **Usage**: Indicates two actions happening at the same time (simultaneously).
    
- **Example**: _I was making dinner; **meanwhile**, he was setting the table._
    
- **Example**: _The children were playing outside; **meanwhile**, the adults were inside talking._
    

---

## **7. Furthermore**

- **Usage**: Adds emphasis or additional information.
    
- **Example**: _She is an excellent singer; **furthermore**, she is a talented dancer._
    
- **Example**: _He is well-respected in his field; **furthermore**, he is a great mentor._
    

---

## **8. Indeed**

- **Usage**: Emphasizes a point or confirms a statement, similar to "in fact."
    
- **Example**: _He is, **indeed**, the best candidate for the job._
    
- **Example**: _The movie was, **indeed**, quite entertaining._
    

---

## **9. On the other hand**

- **Usage**: Indicates a contrasting point or perspective.
    
- **Example**: _She enjoys the beach; **on the other hand**, I prefer the mountains._
    
- **Example**: _The city is exciting; **on the other hand**, it can be overwhelming._
    

---

## **10. In addition**

- **Usage**: Adds further information or another point.
    
- **Example**: _He is a great cook; **in addition**, he is a talented artist._
    
- **Example**: _The movie was entertaining; **in addition**, it was very thought-provoking._
    

---

## **11. As a result**

- **Usage**: Indicates the outcome or consequence of an action.
    
- **Example**: _The company cut its budget; **as a result**, many employees lost their jobs._
    
- **Example**: _She trained for weeks; **as a result**, she ran the marathon successfully._
    

---

## **12. In fact**

- **Usage**: Emphasizes the truth or reality of a statement.
    
- **Example**: _He is not just a friend; **in fact**, he is like family to me._
    
- **Example**: _The meeting was not boring; **in fact**, it was quite informative._
    

---

## **13. Accordingly**

- **Usage**: Indicates that something is done based on or in accordance with something else.
    
- **Example**: _The forecast predicted rain; **accordingly**, the event was moved indoors._
    
- **Example**: _He was given a promotion; **accordingly**, his salary was raised._
    

---

## **14. Nevertheless**

- **Usage**: Indicates a contrast or contradiction, similar to "however."
    
- **Example**: _It was raining; **nevertheless**, they decided to go for a walk._
    
- **Example**: _He didn’t have much experience; **nevertheless**, he got the job._
    

---

## **15. Still**

- **Usage**: Indicates contrast or unexpected continuity.
    
- **Example**: _The team played well; **still**, they lost the game._
    
- **Example**: _It was late, **still**, they kept working._
    

---

## **16. Specifically**

- **Usage**: Provides precise or detailed information.
    
- **Example**: _I want to go to a place with a beach, **specifically** somewhere quiet and relaxing._
    
- **Example**: _We need to focus on one area, **specifically** customer service._
    

---

## **17. Hence**

- **Usage**: Indicates a conclusion or result, similar to "therefore" or "thus."
    
- **Example**: _The train was delayed; **hence**, we missed the meeting._
    
- **Example**: _She had no experience; **hence**, she wasn’t hired for the job._
    

---

## **18. For example**

- **Usage**: Introduces an example to clarify a point.
    
- **Example**: _There are many outdoor activities, **for example**, hiking and biking._
    
- **Example**: _He is very talented, **for example**, he can play the guitar and the piano._
    

---

## **19. On the contrary**

- **Usage**: Used to indicate the opposite or contradict a previous statement.
    
- **Example**: _I thought she would be upset, but **on the contrary**, she was happy._
    
- **Example**: _He is not lazy; **on the contrary**, he works very hard._
    

---

## **20. Otherwise**

- **Usage**: Indicates a result or consequence if something is not done.
    
- **Example**: _You must submit the report by tomorrow; **otherwise**, you’ll miss the deadline._
    
- **Example**: _We should leave now; **otherwise**, we’ll be late._
    

---

### **How to Use Conjunctive Adverbs in Speech**

1. **To Show Cause and Effect**:  
    Use conjunctive adverbs like **therefore**, **thus**, or **as a result** to indicate consequences.  
    **Example**: _I forgot my keys; **therefore**, I couldn’t get inside._
    
2. **To Show Contrast**:  
    Use **however**, **on the other hand**, or **nevertheless** to present a contrast between two clauses.  
    **Example**: _I wanted to go out; **however**, it started raining._
    
3. **To Add Information**:  
    Use **moreover**, **furthermore**, or **in addition** to provide more details or emphasize an idea.  
    **Example**: _He is a good cook; **furthermore**, he’s a great baker._
    
4. **To Show Time or Sequence**:  
    Use **meanwhile**, **then**, or **afterward** to show the timing of events.  
    **Example**: _We went to the store; **meanwhile**, he made dinner._
    

---

### **Tips for Using Conjunctive Adverbs Effectively**

- **Punctuation**: Conjunctive adverbs are often separated by semicolons or periods when they connect two independent clauses. After the adverb, use a comma.  
    **Example**: _She was tired; **however**, she finished her work._
    
- **Vary Your Use**: Use different conjunctive adverbs to avoid repetition and make your writing or speech more engaging.
    
- **Ensure Logical Flow**: Choose the right conjunctive adverb based on the relationship you want to show between the clauses (cause, contrast, addition, etc.).
